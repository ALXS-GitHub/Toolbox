# Pulsar Xlite V4

This is my current mouse, and the one I reach for every day. There was a time when gaming mice competed on how many buttons they could cram onto the shell and how heavy and "premium" they felt in the hand. That era is over. The competitive FPS community figured out years ago that lighter mice reduce fatigue during long sessions and allow faster, more controlled micro-adjustments when aiming. The Pulsar Xlite V4, weighing roughly 54 grams, sits at the leading edge of that ultra-lightweight philosophy without making the compromises that plagued earlier attempts at featherweight mice: no honeycomb holes in the shell, no flimsy build quality, no corners cut on the sensor.

The shape is an ergonomic right-hand design optimized for palm grip, with a curvature that fills the hand naturally and keeps the wrist at a neutral angle. After hours of use, the absence of strain is the thing you notice most. It does not demand a specific grip style, but palm grip users will find it fits like it was molded for them.

## Sensor and Switches

Inside is the XS-1 sensor, Pulsar's collaboration built on the PixArt flagship platform. It tracks at up to 32,000 DPI with 750 IPS maximum speed and 50g of acceleration tolerance, which in practical terms means the sensor will never be the reason you miss a shot. Lift-off distance is adjustable across three stages (2 mm, 1 mm, and 0.7 mm) so you can tune how quickly tracking cuts out when you pick up the mouse to reposition, a detail that matters more than most people realize until they experience it dialed in correctly.

The main buttons use optical switches, which actuate by interrupting a beam of light rather than through metal-on-metal contact. The immediate benefit is the elimination of double-clicking, an issue that has plagued traditional mechanical micro-switches for as long as they have existed in mice. Pulsar goes a step further by pre-sorting switches into three click weight stages, so there is a tactile consistency to every press that does not degrade over time.

## Wireless Without Compromise

Connectivity is 2.4 GHz wireless by default, with an optional 8K polling rate dongle available for those who want to push latency even lower. A wired mode is there as a fallback, though the wireless performance is strong enough that the cable mostly serves as a charging solution. The Pulsar Fusion Drive software handles DPI stages, lift-off distance, button mapping, and polling rate configuration, functional and straightforward without the bloat that plagues some competitors' software suites.

What makes the Xlite V4 worth highlighting is not any single specification, but the way everything works together. The weight, the shape, the sensor precision, the switch reliability, the wireless latency: none of them are the best-in-class by a dramatic margin, but none of them are a weak link either. It is a mouse with no asterisks.
